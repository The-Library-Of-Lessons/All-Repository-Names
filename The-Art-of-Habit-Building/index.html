The Foundation

What Is It?

A habit is a routine or behavior that is performed regularly and, in many cases, automatically. It is the brain's way of conserving energy by turning a sequence of actions into an automatic routine. The process of habit formation can be broken down into a simple four-step loop: cue, craving, response, and reward.

The Necessity

Why It Matters

📈

Compound Growth

Small improvements of 1% each day lead to massive results over time. Habits are the vehicle for long-term transformation.

🔋

Energy Efficiency

By automating routine tasks, habits free up cognitive energy for more complex decision-making and creative work.

👤

Identity Shifting

Every action you take is a vote for the type of person you wish to become. Your habits are how you embody your identity.

🎯

Consistency

Habits provide a reliable structure that ensures progress even on days when motivation is low.

The Skills

The Pillars of Mastery

The Habit Loop

Understand the mechanics of Cue (trigger), Craving (motivation), Response (action), and Reward (satisfaction).

Environment Design

Make the cues of good habits obvious and the cues of bad habits invisible. Your environment shapes your behavior.

Identity-Based Habits

Focus on who you want to become, not what you want to achieve. The goal is not to run a marathon, but to become a runner.

Proof It Works

Case Studies

💬
British Cycling — Marginal Gains
Case Study
›

Dave Brailsford transformed British cycling by looking for 1% improvements in everything—from bike seats to pillows. These tiny improvements compounded into Olympic dominance.

The Lesson: You don't need radical changes to see radical results. Small, consistent improvements in your daily habits are more powerful than one-off transformations.

💬
The 'Paper Clip' Strategy
Case Study
›

A young stockbroker moved 120 paper clips from one jar to another every time he made a sales call. This visual progress cue made the habit of calling addictive and led to his massive success.

The Lesson: Visual cues and tracking provide immediate feedback and satisfaction, which are crucial for reinforcing the habit loop.

The Big Picture

Summary of All Lessons

L01

Make it obvious. Use implementation intentions: 'I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION].'

L02

Make it attractive. Use temptation bundling: Pair an action you want to do with an action you need to do.

L03

Make it easy. Use the Two-Minute Rule: When you start a new habit, it should take less than two minutes to do.

L04

Make it satisfying. Give yourself an immediate reward when you complete your habit to reinforce the loop.

L05

Invert the laws for bad habits. To break a bad habit: Make it invisible, unattractive, difficult, and unsatisfying.

L06

Never miss twice. Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new habit. Get back on track immediately.

L07

Focus on systems, not goals. Goals are about the results you want to achieve. Systems are about the processes that lead to those results.

L08

Design your environment. Be the architect of your surroundings. Prime your environment for future use.

L09

The Goldilocks Rule. Maintain motivation by working on tasks of 'just right' difficulty—not too easy, not too hard.

L10

Habit Stacking. Identify a current habit you already have and then stack your new behavior on top of it.

"You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems."
