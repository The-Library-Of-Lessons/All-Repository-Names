The Foundation

What Is It?

An Atomic Habit is a regular practice or routine that is not only small and easy to do but also the source of incredible power; a component of the system of compound growth. Bad habits repeat themselves again and again not because you don't want to change, but because you have the wrong system for change. You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems.

The Necessity

Why It Matters

📈

The Power of Compounding

Small 1% improvements every day compound into massive results over time. If you get 1% better each day for one year, you'll end up thirty-seven times better.

🎭

Identity-Based Habits

The most effective way to change your habits is to focus not on what you want to achieve, but on who you wish to become.

⛓️

The Habit Loop

Every habit follows a four-step pattern: Cue, Craving, Response, and Reward. Mastering this loop is key to behavior change.

🛑

Breaking Bad Patterns

By inverting the Four Laws of Behavior Change, you can make bad habits invisible, unattractive, difficult, and unsatisfying.

The Skills

The Pillars of Mastery

Make It Obvious

Design your environment to make the cues of your good habits visible. Use implementation intentions and habit stacking.

Make It Attractive

Use temptation bundling and join a culture where your desired behavior is the normal behavior to increase craving.

Make It Easy

Reduce friction. Prime your environment for future use. Master the 2-Minute Rule: any habit can be started in less than two minutes.

Proof It Works

Case Studies

💬
British Cycling — Marginal Gains
Case Study
›

Sir Dave Brailsford took over a failing team and applied the 'aggregation of marginal gains'—improving everything related to cycling by 1%. Within five years, they dominated the Tour de France.

The Lesson: Success is the product of daily habits—not once-in-a-lifetime transformations. The system is more important than the goal.

💬
The Hospital Handwashing Study
Case Study
›

A hospital improved handwashing compliance by simply making the sinks more visible and accessible. No complex training was needed; just environmental design.

The Lesson: Environment is the invisible hand that shapes human behavior. We often follow the path of least resistance.

The Big Picture

Summary of All Lessons

L01

Focus on systems, not goals. Goals are about the results you want to achieve. Systems are about the processes that lead to those results.

L02

Build identity-based habits. Decide the type of person you want to be, then prove it to yourself with small wins.

L03

The 1% Rule. Don't aim for radical change. Aim to be 1% better every day. Small improvements are the secret to long-term success.

L04

Use Habit Stacking. Link a new habit to a current one. 'After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].'

L05

Environment over Willpower. Be the architect of your environment. Make the cues of good habits obvious and the cues of bad habits invisible.

L06

Master the 2-Minute Rule. When you start a new habit, it should take less than two minutes to do. A habit must be established before it can be improved.

L07

Reduce Friction. Make it so easy to do the right thing that you'll do it even when you don't feel like it.

L08

Never miss twice. Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new habit. Get back on track immediately.

L09

Use a Habit Tracker. Don't break the chain. Visual evidence of your progress provides immediate satisfaction.

L10

The Goldilocks Rule. Humans experience peak motivation when working on tasks that are right on the edge of their current abilities.
